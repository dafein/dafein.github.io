The new patient revolution is among us…

February 1, 2014self-efficacy, the network effectRoni Zeiger

So says physician Emma Hill in her piece called “Smart patients” in the current issue of Lancet Oncology. (Free registration required to see the full text.) Emma asked us if she could join Smart Patients to study how cancer patients are using the Smart Patients online community.

We are daily witnesses to insightful and moving conversations between patients and caregivers as they help each other learn about their disease and put it into the context of their own experience. Emma’s reaction: “It is incredible to see how a patient recently diagnosed with a very rare cancer can, the same day, communicate with someone else with the same disease.”

The Smart Patients team has another big goal, in addition to helping patients and caregivers learn from each other. We also want to help them teach the physicians and researchers charged with taking better care of them. The Lancet piece is perhaps a bit bolder:

“The new patient revolution is among us. We, the doctors, might think we are the source of all knowledge, but the age-old model of consulting with your family and friends is now global, with a growing membership to share advice. It is not a replacement doctor, far from it, and the moderators are on hand to guide members. It is a reminder that patients are taking power of their disease, and are educating each other through improved international communication. It should humbly remind us that every patient is an expert in their own chosen field, namely themselves and their own life.”

It seems there’s something in the air, at least that being breathed by our friends in the UK — this week BMJ also published a piece called “Towards the patient revolution”.

In their own efforts to partner with patients, BMJ is considering asking authors of research articles to explain how they involved patients in the design of their study. Tyranny of the commons? No, more like tapping into the most underutilized resource in health care.

It’s an exciting privilege for us to be part of the transition from patients as passive recipients of care and passive participants in clinical trials, to patients as true collaborators.
