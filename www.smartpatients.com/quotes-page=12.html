  • There has been a lot of discussion about the role of IL-2 in modern cancer treatment. What we do know is that it offers a Complete Response in only a very small number of patients (probably around 5-10%) but, in most of those patients who do get a Complete Response, there is genuine hope they will not relapse and may live decades or more in remission - not a guaranteed "cure" but the closest we can get at the moment. Indeed there are people who were treated in the 80s and 90s still alive and in remission today thanks to IL-2.

  • Checkpoints are protein molecules or their receptors, each located on the surface of cells. When these pairs interlock, they block some part of a process of cellular interaction. Blocking an immune checkpoint puts the brakes on the immune system cell so it won't attack the other cell. This is called an immune checkpoint blockade. A drug that links with either side of the checkpoint is called an immune checkpoint blockade inhibitor because it stops the immune cell from being blocked in its attack. PD-1 and PDL-1 are the two sides of one already-known immune checkpoint. Experimental drugs such as nivolumab and pembrolizumab successfully pair with either the PD-1 molecule or the receptor PD-L1. Either prevents the immune system from being blocked at that checkpoint.
